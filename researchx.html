Research topics

Our research deals with answers to fundamental questions as well as practical applications in the context of random matrix theory, complex networks and quantum information theory. A non-exhaustive list of topics might include:

  • RMT and quantum transport
  • Spectral and eigenfunction properpies of networks
  • Estimation of eigenvalues, quantum tomography
  • Properties of real networks
Our favorite tool for research is computer simulation, so usually there is a lot of code. Below, we've provided brief blurbs for several present and future research directions.

Groups and RMT

The groups O(N) and U(N) as invariant ensembles have become an important reference in the study of broad classes of quantum systems, as in nuclear physics, quantum chaos and mesoscopic quantum transport and more recently in quantum information. We are interested in the integration of elements over these classical groups, as a way to compute probabilities in projective measurements. Also, we are interested in the ergodicity hypothesis for random matrices, as a way to study self-averages over these groups. Our results can be applied to phenomena of universal conductance fluctuations and even to identify local regions with RMT properpies.

Quantum Tomography

Quantum state tomography deals with the complete characterization of an ensemble of identically and independently distributed quantum states. It involves the estimation of all elements of the density matrix describing that ensemble of states. Our approach is to use the overlap between two mixed quantum states, which is based on the fact that the distribution of overlaps between a given density matrix and its image under random unitary transformations depends only on the eigenvalues of the density matrix. We have a strategy to estimate this quantity which heavily depends on the knowledge of the joint probability distribution of projection probabilities for random orthonormal quantum states.

Complex Networks

Complex networked systems are made up of many units (be them individuals, atoms, genes, plants, etc.) that interact among them following often non-trivial patterns. These patterns can be mapped into complex networks. We started this journey by analysing the similarity between networks, without considering any physical space. That said, in general, networks are not embedded in any physical or geographical space, but often, we need to preserve the spatial component of the interaction networks, so here we are interested in such systems. Our models are extensions of the so called random rectangular graphs.
